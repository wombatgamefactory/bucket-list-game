# Bucket List: Australian Birds — Current Rules State

## Overview

Bucket List: Australian Birds is a gateway-weight card drafting and grid-building game for 2-5 players. Players draft bird cards from a shared market and place them into a personal 6×5 grid, trying to create runs of 4 adjacent cards sharing a symbol to score points. Each card shows a real Australian bird with a habitat symbol and a diet symbol. An optional "Field Notes" bonus rewards players who have seen the birds in real life.

**Series:** Part of the Bucket List series. Other editions include Bucket List: London (city/tourist edition). The core mechanism is shared across editions with thematically appropriate categories.

**Target audience:** Families, casual gamers, tourists, birdwatchers. BGG weight ~1.5-2.0.

**Tagline:** "From rainforest to reef, collect them all."

## Components

### Bird Cards (100 unique)

Each bird card shows:
- Bird name
- Photorealistic bird image
- Habitat symbol (1 of 5, or WILD)
- Diet symbol (1 of 5, or WILD)
- A 60-character interesting fact about the bird
- A tick box (for the Field Notes variant — see below)

**Card types:**
- **Standard cards (90):** 1 habitat + 1 diet. Distributed across 25 habitat/diet combinations.
- **Wild-Diet cards (5):** 1 habitat + wild diet (matches any diet). One per habitat.
- **Wild-Habitat cards (5):** Wild habitat (matches any habitat) + 1 diet. One per diet.

### The 10 Categories

**Habitats (5):**
1. Rainforest
2. Bushland & Scrub
3. Wetlands & Waterways
4. Coastal & Ocean
5. Outback & Grassland

**Diets (5):**
1. Seeds & Grain
2. Nectar & Fruit
3. Insects & Invertebrates
4. Fish & Aquatic Prey
5. Meat & Carrion

### Other Components
- Category icon reference cards
- Rulebook
- Score pad (optional)

**NOTE:** Header cards have been REMOVED from the design. The earlier version used header cards placed along grid edges; these were cut to simplify the game. Scoring is now purely based on adjacency runs of matching symbols within the grid, with no header requirement.

## Setup

1. Shuffle all 100 bird cards.
2. Build the play deck: take **20 cards per player** from the shuffled stack to form the deck (e.g. 60 cards for 3 players, 80 cards for 4 players, 100 cards for 5 players). Set any remaining cards aside, unseen, out of the game.
3. Deal a market of 5 face-up cards in the centre of the table from the play deck.
4. Each player needs space for a 6×5 grid (personal tableau — 6 cards wide, 5 cards high).

---

## Turn Structure

On your turn:

1. **Draft:** Take 1 card from the market.
2. **Place:** Place the card in your personal 6×5 grid. Cards must be placed adjacent (orthogonally) to at least one existing card. Your first card can go anywhere in your grid space.
3. **Refill:** The market refills to 5 cards from the deck (if any remain).

Players take turns clockwise. Every player drafts and places the same number of cards.

### Market Refresh Rule

If 3 or more of the 5 market cards share the same symbol (habitat or diet), the active player MAY clear the entire market and refill it from the deck before drafting. This is optional — the player might want those cards.

---

## Game End

The game ends when the deck runs out **and** all cards in the market have been taken. Because the deck is built at exactly 20 cards per player, every player ends up with exactly 20 cards, filling 20 of the 30 cells in their 6×5 grid. The 10 empty cells are strategic — players deliberately choose which cells to leave empty.

---

## Scoring

### Primary Scoring: Runs of 4

Check every row and every column of your grid. Score 1 point for every run of 4 adjacent cards that share the same symbol (habitat or diet).

**Overlapping runs:** A row of 5 matching adjacent cards contains two overlapping runs of 4 (positions 1-4 and 2-5), scoring 2 points. A row of 6 matching adjacent cards contains three overlapping runs, scoring 3 points.

**Both symbols count independently.** A single row might score for both a habitat run AND a diet run.

**Wild cards:** A wild habitat matches ANY habitat symbol for run purposes. A wild diet matches ANY diet symbol. Wilds are flexible but contested — everyone wants them.

**Empty cells break runs.** A gap in a row or column stops a run from continuing across it.

### Maximum Theoretical Score

With 5 rows + 6 columns = 11 lines. Each row (6 cards wide) can score up to 3 runs of 4; each column (5 cards high) can score up to 2 runs of 4. Both symbols count independently, so theoretical maxima compound. Realistic good scores cluster around 10-20 points.

## Field Notes (Optional Bucket List Variant)

This is the thematic heart of the game and what makes it uniquely "Bucket List."

### The Tick System

Each bird card has a small tick box. Over the lifetime of the physical game, the owner marks (ticks) birds they have personally seen in real life. These ticks are permanent — they accumulate over time as the owner spots more birds. The game literally grows with its owner.

### Field Notes Scoring

At game end, each row and column in your grid scores **1 bonus point** if every card in that line is ticked (has been seen by the owner in real life). Empty rows/columns don't score. A line with a single ticked card and no other cards still scores the bonus.

### How This Scales

- **New copy (0% ticked):** Field Notes is dormant. The game is purely the symbol-matching grid game.
- **Partially ticked (~50%):** Field Notes creates interesting tension — players might draft ticked cards even if the symbol match isn't ideal.
- **Heavily ticked (~100%):** Field Notes becomes a secondary scoring axis rewarding dense grid filling, competing against the symbol-matching objective.

This mechanic means the game is different every time based on who owns it and how many birds they've spotted.

## Design Principles & History

### What was removed and why
- **Header cards:** Originally players placed header cards along grid edges to define what categories scored in each row/column. Removed to simplify — scoring is now purely adjacency-based.
- **Triple-category cards (3 symbols):** Originally 10 cards had 2 habitats + 1 diet or 1 habitat + 2 diets. Removed as too confusing; those birds were reassigned as standard 2-category cards.
- **Powers/special actions:** Tested and removed as too complex for the target weight. Jump, Reserve, Swap, and Rearrange powers all proved too deep for a gateway game.
- **Double-scoring rows/columns race:** The first player to complete a row/column could flip a header for 2x scoring. Removed along with headers.
- **End-of-turn market discard:** Players could discard 1 market card at end of turn to refresh. Removed — the 3-of-same market refresh rule alone handles market health, and removing the discard keeps the turn structure cleaner.

### What survived and works
- **Drafting from shared market:** Creates interaction without direct conflict.
- **Adjacency placement constraint:** Cards must connect, creating a spatial puzzle.
- **Run-of-4 scoring:** Simple, scannable, satisfying.
- **Wild cards:** Add flexibility without complexity.
- **Field Notes / bucket list mechanic:** The unique hook — real-world experience matters.
- **Market refresh rule (3-of-same clears):** Prevents market stagnation.
- **Player-scaled deck (20 cards × player count):** Guarantees every player gets exactly 20 cards and equal turns, regardless of player count.

### Shelf Neighbours
The game sits between Cascadia (heavier, more thematic) and Point Salad (lighter, abstract). Closest comparisons: Cascadia (personal tableau, habitat scoring, animal theme), Castle Combo (grid with neighbour scoring), Point Salad (scoring rules as drafted components).

## Art Direction

- **Bird cards:** Photorealistic wildlife photography style, each bird centred in its natural habitat, shot with a 300mm telephoto lens aesthetic.
- **Category icons:** Japanese animation style (anime/game art), bold dark brown outlines, flat colour fills with cel-shading, contained within oval frames.
- **Card backs:** Lush photorealistic composition of multiple colourful Australian birds on eucalyptus branches, with "Bucket List: Australian Birds" title.
- **Overall aesthetic:** Premium, collectable, celebratory of Australian birdlife.
